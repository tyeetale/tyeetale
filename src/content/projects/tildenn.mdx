## Founder-Market Fit

I'd just graduated from NYU Shanghai — a university that by definition required crossing borders. By 22, I'd been to over 22 countries. I was living in Thailand, one of the world's travel epicenters, running one of the largest travel communities online as an admin of Discord Travelers, and obsessively documenting places as a Level 7 Google Maps contributor. Travel wasn't a market I was entering — it was a life I was already living.

Growing up, I was always drawn to products that used technology to navigate the physical world. Foursquare's check-ins, Ingress's geofencing, Pokemon Go's AR layer — systems that bridged digital intelligence with real places. I wanted to build something in that lineage: technology that makes the physical world more explorable.

## Context (2022)

In early 2022, [global travel was surging back](https://www.unwto.org/tourism-data/global-and-regional-tourism-performance) post-COVID. Borders were reopening, revenge travel was trending. Simultaneously, OpenAI had opened [GPT-3 to developers](https://openai.com/blog/gpt-3-apps) — hundreds of apps were building on the API, but almost none were tackling consumer travel. The timing was right: a problem I'd felt my entire life finally had a technical surface area I could build on.

## Problem & Insight

After conducting 50+ interviews with travelers, a pattern emerged. People planned trips in shared spreadsheets — one person doing hours of research, Googling restaurants, cross-referencing distances, building day-by-day schedules by hand. The tools available were either too rigid (packaged tours) or too scattered (10 browser tabs of half-formed research). There was no intelligent system that could handle the research burden while keeping the planner in control.

The target user was specific: the traveler who wants to spend every waking minute immersed in culture, exploring rapidly, doing as much as possible. Not the resort lounger — the hyper-adventurer who needs a system as ambitious as they are.

## System Design

GPT-3 in 2022 could not generate trustworthy suggestions. It hallucinated places, invented addresses, and had no concept of travel logistics. You couldn't just wrap a transformer and ship it. This was before MCP, before RAG, before skills or context engineering frameworks existed. I was solving the problem of grounding AI in real-world data from scratch.

The core challenge was turning "place data" — the richest, most contextual type of information — into actionable, pre-planned itineraries. Not just one event, but a full agenda of balanced activities with timing, proximity, and variety constraints. Like applying Dijkstra's algorithm to lived experience.

I built a hybrid system:
- **GPT-3** handled natural language input and initial ideation
- **A custom decision-making ruleset** (independent of the LLM) handled the actual itinerary logic
- **Hierarchical clustering with haversine distance** grouped activities by proximity
- **Type-aware rules** ensured variety — no back-to-back restaurants, balanced categories across days, realistic timing between locations

Every suggestion was validated against Google Places API and Mapbox for ground truth. But Places API doesn't do contextual suggestion mapping — it gives you raw data, not relevance. I had to brute-force the right results by engineering queries that returned culturally relevant, non-generic places. This was context engineering before the concept had a name.

The algorithm ran on AWS Lambda functions. The main app was Next.js with MongoDB for the database, auth, and API orchestration.

The key insight: use the right tool at the right time. Transformers for language understanding. Classical ML for spatial clustering. Rule engines for logical constraints. The AI wasn't the product — the system was.

## Product & Design

The differentiator was how users interacted with the output. I built three synchronized views from scratch: a custom calendar, a map with clustered pins, and a day-by-day agenda. Users could see their trip across all three dimensions simultaneously — drag an activity on the calendar and watch it move on the map. The AI re-optimized around manual changes.

Multiple product iterations were prototyped and discarded before landing on this multi-view model that worked across desktop and mobile. The design philosophy: the system does the heavy lifting, the human stays in control.

## Outcome

Solo-built over two years as an indie developer in Thailand. Reached ~100 DAU. The product worked — users loved the planning experience. But the business model didn't pencil: content/affiliate monetization couldn't sustain growth, and direct charging for trip planning was a hard sell in a market conditioned to free tools.

I made the call to sunset Tildenn and pursue a larger opportunity at Blue Origin. As a solo 0-to-1 build — user research, system architecture, ML pipeline, product design, growth, all one person — it became the foundation everything after was built on.
